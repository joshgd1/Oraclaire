# Oraclaire Stakeholder and User Persona Analysis

Date: 2026-05-13
Purpose: Unvarnished analysis of who buys, uses, resists, and benefits from a workplace burnout detection and wellbeing scoring system.

---

## 1. BUYER Personas — Who Signs the Check

### 1A. CHRO / VP People (Enterprise, 1000+ employees)

**What they tell the board:** "We are investing in employee wellbeing."

**What they actually want:**

- A defensible narrative for why turnover is or is not their fault
- Quantifiable data to present at board meetings (dashboards, trends, scores)
- Early warning on flight risks for high-value employees before resignations hit
- Something to point to during litigation or regulatory inquiry: "We had a system in place"
- A differentiator in employer branding / Glassware reviews / recruitment

**What they fear:**

- A data breach that exposes individual employee stress scores to the wrong people
- The tool generating evidence that the organization IS toxic (discovery risk)
- Being asked "so what do we DO about it?" and having no answer beyond the dashboard
- Spending six figures on something employees refuse to use

**Buying trigger:** CEO asks "why are we losing people?" OR a competitor launches a wellbeing initiative OR an employee burnout incident makes it to HR's attention (medical leave, exit interview pattern, or public complaint).

**Budget source:** Often pulled from "employee experience" or "benefits" budget, not from IT. This is important: the buyer views this as a benefits spend, not a software procurement.

**Honest assessment:** This persona genuinely wants better outcomes, but their incentive structure rewards the appearance of action more than action itself. A tool that produces beautiful dashboards but changes nothing about workload will be renewed if it generates good optics. The product's real value to this persona is narrative control.

---

### 1B. HR Director (Mid-Market, 100-1000 employees)

**What they tell the CEO:** "This will help us retain people."

**What they actually want:**

- Something affordable that does not require a full-time admin
- A way to show the CEO/COO that "people stuff" is being handled professionally
- Answers to "why is [department] having so many issues?" without relying on gossip
- Reduced time spent on exit interviews that all say the same thing
- A tool that makes them look proactive, not reactive

**What they fear:**

- Being the person who bought the thing nobody uses (waste perception)
- Managers pushing back: "Why are you monitoring my team?"
- Employees viewing it as surveillance and losing trust in HR
- The tool telling them something they cannot fix (e.g., "your CEO is the problem")

**Buying trigger:** A cluster of resignations from one team, a disability claim related to stress, or the board/owner asking "what are we doing about burnout?"

**Budget source:** Operating budget, often competing with training spend, perks, or that Slack upgrade nobody asked for. Price sensitivity is real at this tier.

**Honest assessment:** This persona is the most likely to genuinely care about employee wellbeing AND have the least power to change the conditions causing burnout. They will buy the tool hoping it gives them ammunition to push leadership toward change. Sometimes it works; often it does not. The product needs to give this persona leverage, not just data.

---

### 1C. CEO / COO (SMB, under 100 employees)

**What they tell themselves:** "We take care of our people."

**What they actually want:**

- To stop being surprised by resignations
- To feel like they are a "good employer" without fundamentally changing how they run the business
- A simple answer to a messy problem
- If external/client-facing: a talking point for prospects who ask about company culture

**What they fear:**

- The tool telling them they are the problem
- Spending money on "soft" stuff with no clear ROI
- Employees thinking the company does not trust them
- Complexity. If it takes more than 15 minutes to understand, it will not get bought

**Buying trigger:** A key employee quits and cites burnout. Or a peer at a networking event mentions using something similar.

**Budget source:** Direct P&L. Every dollar is visible. This persona will compare the subscription cost to "just talking to people more" and wonder why they cannot do the latter.

**Honest assessment:** This persona will buy if the pain of losing someone is fresh. They will not renew if the tool requires behavioral change they are unwilling to make. The product must deliver an obvious, quick win within the first month or the subscription lapses.

---

### 1D. Benefits Broker / Consultant

**What they tell their client:** "This is a best-in-class wellbeing solution."

**What they actually want:**

- Another product in their portfolio that generates recurring commission
- Something they can demo easily that makes them look knowledgeable
- A vendor who makes them look good (responsive, professional, easy to work with)
- Something with a recognizable brand or compelling pitch deck they can borrow

**What they fear:**

- Recommending something that blows up (data leak, PR incident, employee backlash)
- The vendor being hard to reach when the client has questions
- The product being too complex for them to explain in a 15-minute call

**Buying trigger:** A client asks "what are other companies doing about burnout?" OR the broker sees competitors offering wellbeing tools and wants parity.

**Budget source:** Not their money. They are channel. They care about ease of sale and commission structure.

**Honest assessment:** This persona does not care about employee wellbeing. They care about portfolio breadth and commission. If the product is easy to sell and generates reliable revenue for them, they will push it regardless of efficacy. This is a distribution play, not a values alignment.

## 2. USER Personas — Who Actually Uses It Daily

### 2A. HR/People Team Member Monitoring Org Health

**Daily behavior:** Logs in Monday morning. Checks overall org score. Drills into departments that moved. Flags any individual alerts (if the system generates them). Pulls a report for the weekly leadership meeting.

**What they need:**

- A score or trend that is easy to explain to non-HR people ("Engineering is at 62, down from 71 last month")
- Actionable signals, not vague heat maps ("Three people in Sales flagged high risk this week" is useful; "Sales is yellow" is not)
- A way to track whether interventions worked (did the team offsite actually move the needle?)
- Exportable reports that look professional in a PowerPoint

**What frustrates them:**

- False positives (the tool says someone is burning out; they are on vacation)
- No clear next action (the score changed, but what do I DO?)
- Having to defend the tool's accuracy to skeptical managers
- Data that is too granular (feels like surveillance) or too aggregated (not actionable)

**Frequency of use:** 2-3 times per week in steady state. Daily during a "situation" (restructuring, post-layoff, seasonal crunch).

**Honest assessment:** This is the primary daily user and the product's success or failure lives or dies with them. If the tool makes their job harder without making them more effective, they will quietly stop logging in and the renewal dies. They need the tool to make them look competent, not to give them more work.

---

### 2B. Team Manager Checking Team Signals

**Daily behavior:** Gets a notification or email digest. Glances at team score. Maybe clicks into one or two names if something looks off. Mostly ignores it unless there is an alert.

**What they need:**

- A simple, glanceable summary (email, Slack message, or single dashboard tile)
- Clear language, not clinical jargon ("your team is showing elevated stress signals" not "psychosocial risk factor 0.73")
- Suggested conversation starters or actions (not "here is a number" but "consider checking in with [name] this week")
- Plausible deniability. They want to be able to say they are monitoring without feeling like they are spying

**What frustrates them:**

- Feeling like they are being asked to be a therapist
- Alerts with no context (why is this person flagged?)
- The tool telling them something they already know but cannot fix ("Yes, I know my team is burned out. We are understaffed by two people and I have no hiring budget.")
- Being held accountable for scores they cannot control

**Frequency of use:** Weekly at best. Many will engage only when prompted or during 1:1 prep.

**Honest assessment:** This persona is the hardest to win. Most managers did not become managers because they love managing people problems. The tool is asking them to do emotional labor they may not be equipped for or interested in. The product must either (a) make the interaction feel like a normal management activity, not a therapy session, or (b) route the signal back to HR/support functions rather than dumping it on the manager.

---

### 2C. Individual Employee Checking Own Wellbeing

**Daily behavior:** Opens the app/portal when prompted (maybe). Takes a pulse check (if short). Maybe looks at their own trend. Almost never reads resources/articles.

**What they need:**

- To feel like the tool is FOR them, not about them
- Privacy. Absolute certainty that their individual data is not visible to their manager
- Something quick. Under 60 seconds per interaction
- Tangible help (access to counseling, schedule flexibility, workload adjustment) not just a score that says "you are stressed"
- Control over what gets shared and what stays private

**What frustrates them:**

- Feeling surveilled. If the tool pulls data from email/calendar/work patterns without clear consent, trust is gone immediately
- Being told they are burned out by a machine while the company does nothing about the cause
- The "wellness washing" perception: the company bought a tool instead of reducing workload
- Gamification. Being asked to "improve your wellbeing score" when the stressor is systemic

**Frequency of use:** Initial spike (curiosity). Drops to near-zero unless the experience is genuinely valuable and private. Pulse checks get skipped. Notifications get dismissed.

**Honest assessment:** This is the persona the product CLAIMS to serve but the one with the least buying power. Their engagement is voluntary and fragile. Most employee wellbeing tools see 20-30% adoption in month one, declining to 5-10% by month six. The product must deliver individual value that is obvious and immediate, or this persona becomes a ghost town that undermines the entire value proposition. The uncomfortable question: does the product work if employees do not engage? If the answer is "it uses passive data signals," the privacy backlash will be severe. If the answer is "it requires active engagement," the dropout problem will be severe.

---

### 2D. Executive Looking at Dashboards

**Daily behavior:** Never logs into the product directly. Receives a monthly summary email or sees a slide in a quarterly business review. May ask one question about a trend. Moves on.

**What they need:**

- One number or trend line they can track over time
- Benchmarking against their industry or prior quarters
- Confidence that the number means something real
- The ability to say "we are monitoring this" to the board

**What frustrates them:**

- Complexity. They will not learn a new interface for this
- Bad news with no proposed solution
- Data that contradicts what they hear from their direct reports (trust crisis)

**Frequency of use:** Quarterly for the dashboard. Monthly for the email summary. Never for the app.

**Honest assessment:** This persona will see the product two to four times per year. The entire relationship is mediated through a report someone else prepares. The product's executive reporting must be outstanding or this persona will not even remember it exists.

---

### 2E. Occupational Health / Company Nurse

**Daily behavior:** Uses the tool as an input alongside other health data. May flag individuals for follow-up. References trends during return-to-work planning.

**What they need:**

- Integration with their existing case management workflow (not a separate login)
- Clinical credibility. The scoring model must be defensible
- Longitudinal tracking per individual (with consent)
- Clear boundaries between wellbeing scoring and clinical assessment

**What frustrates them:**

- Being handed data with no clinical context
- The tool overstepping into clinical territory (it is a wellbeing tool, not a diagnostic instrument)
- Duplicates with existing occupational health assessments

**Frequency of use:** Weekly during active cases. Monthly otherwise.

**Honest assessment:** This persona is a secondary user and a credibility validator. If occupational health professionals publicly endorse the tool's methodology, it lends weight. If they dismiss it as "not evidence-based," it undermines the entire product. Get their input early, even if they are not the primary buyer.

## 3. RESISTANCE Personas — Who Will Push Back

### 3A. Privacy-Conscious Employees

**Arguments they will make:**

- "This is surveillance dressed up as wellness."
- "How do I know my manager cannot see my individual data?"
- "The company should fix the root cause, not monitor my stress."
- "What happens to this data if I am laid off? If I sue? If the company is acquired?"

**Power level:** Individually low, collectively very high. One viral Slack message or Glassdoor review about "our company is tracking our stress levels" can kill deployment.

**What would reduce their resistance:**

- Crystal-clear, auditable privacy architecture. Not a policy document. Actual technical guarantees.
- Employee ownership of individual data. They can see exactly what is stored and delete it.
- Opt-in, not opt-out. (This kills adoption metrics but saves the product from backlash.)
- Aggregate-only views for managers. No individual drill-down unless the employee explicitly shares.

**Honest assessment:** Their concerns are legitimate. A wellbeing tool that employees view as surveillance is worse than no tool at all because it erodes trust in the employer and the product category. Privacy cannot be a feature slide. It must be the architectural foundation.

---

### 3B. Union Representatives

**Arguments they will make:**

- "This is management monitoring workers' emotional states."
- "Will this be used in performance management? Disciplinary action? Layoff selection?"
- "We need a collective bargaining agreement addendum before deployment."
- "Who owns the data? Who has access? What are the retention policies?"

**Power level:** High in unionized environments. Can block deployment entirely or demand restrictions that make the product less useful to the buyer.

**What would reduce their resistance:**

- Written guarantees (legally binding, not marketing copy) that data cannot be used for discipline
- Union involvement in deployment design (aggregate thresholds, no individual access)
- Data residency and retention terms that union legal counsel approves
- A track record of responsible use at other unionized employers

**Honest assessment:** Unions are not anti-wellbeing. They are anti-management-having-more-information-about-workers-than-workers-have-about-management. The product must be positioned as a shared resource, not a management tool. If the buyer sees the union as an obstacle to work around, the deployment will fail.

---

### 3C. IT Security Teams

**Arguments they will make:**

- "What data does this collect? Where is it stored? Who processes it?"
- "Does it integrate with our HRIS? What are the API security implications?"
- "Is it SOC 2 certified? ISO 27001? GDPR compliant?"
- "Does it ingest data from email, calendar, Slack, or other communication tools? If so, that is a hard no from us."

**Power level:** Gatekeeper. Can block procurement on security grounds regardless of what HR wants.

**What would reduce their resistance:**

- Compliance certifications ready before the first sales call
- Clear, concise data flow documentation (not a 40-page whitepaper)
- Minimal data collection by design. The less data the product touches, the easier the security review.
- On-premise or private cloud options for regulated industries
- Integration patterns that do not require broad API access

**Honest assessment:** Security teams are doing their job. The product must be designed for minimal data surface from day one. Retroactive security hardening is expensive and slow. Build for the security review from the start.

---

### 3D. Legal / Compliance

**Arguments they will make:**

- "If the tool identifies someone as high-risk and we do not act, does that create liability?"
- "If the tool identifies someone as high-risk and we DO act, does that create liability (perceived discrimination, medical privacy violations)?"
- "Does this data become discoverable in employment litigation?"
- "Are we inadvertently creating a medical record subject to HIPAA/GDPR/ADA requirements?"

**Power level:** Veto authority in regulated industries. Strong influence everywhere else.

**What would reduce their resistance:**

- Clear legal positioning: this is NOT a medical device or clinical assessment
- Data minimization by design (less data = less discovery risk)
- Legal review templates the buyer's counsel can use (do not make them write the legal analysis from scratch)
- Option to disable individual-level scoring entirely and operate in aggregate-only mode

**Honest assessment:** This is the most underappreciated blocker. The moment a company has data that says "Employee X is at high risk of burnout," they have a duty-of-care argument to contend with. The tool creates legal exposure simply by existing. The product must give legal teams a way to manage that exposure, or they will recommend against purchase.

---

### 3E. Managers Who Do Not Want to Deal With "People Problems"

**Arguments they will make (privately):**

- "I have a team to run and deadlines to hit. I cannot also manage people's feelings."
- "This is HR's job, not mine."
- "If someone is burned out, they should talk to me directly, not have a machine tell me."
- "This is going to be used to evaluate my management, isn't it?"

**Power level:** Individually medium. Collectively, they determine whether the tool gets used by teams or ignored.

**What would reduce their resistance:**

- The tool does not evaluate the manager. It surfaces team signals. Period.
- Clear routing: team-level signals go to HR/People, not to the manager's manager. The manager is a partner, not a subject.
- Practical, script-level support ("here is a way to open this conversation" not "you should care more")
- Option for the manager to flag "I am aware and addressing this" to close the loop without detail

**Honest assessment:** This persona will never love the product. The goal is passive tolerance, not enthusiastic adoption. If the tool makes their life slightly easier (e.g., gives them a reason to have a conversation they were avoiding), they will not resist. If it creates new obligations with no support, they will undermine it through inaction.

## 4. The Uncomfortable Truth — Who Actually Benefits?

| Persona                    | Does this person genuinely benefit?                                                     | Or are they a means to an end?                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| CHRO / VP People           | Genuinely benefits from data-driven narrative and early warning                         | But also uses it for career protection and optics. Both can be true.                                                                                                                                                          |
| HR Director                | Genuinely benefits from leverage to push leadership                                     | But the tool cannot fix the systemic issues they face. It gives them evidence, not power.                                                                                                                                     |
| CEO/COO (SMB)              | Benefits if they act on the data                                                        | Means to an end if they buy it for optics and change nothing. Common pattern.                                                                                                                                                 |
| Benefits broker            | Does not benefit from the product's core value                                          | Means to an end (commission). Purely transactional.                                                                                                                                                                           |
| HR team member             | Genuinely benefits if the tool reduces their workload and increases their effectiveness | Means to an end if the tool adds reporting burden without reducing crises.                                                                                                                                                    |
| Team manager               | Marginal benefit                                                                        | Mostly a means to an end. The manager is a distribution channel for the product's value, not a primary beneficiary.                                                                                                           |
| Individual employee        | The stated primary beneficiary                                                          | In practice, the least empowered. Benefits only if (a) the tool gives them real help, (b) the company acts on signals, and (c) privacy is bulletproof. If any of these fail, the employee is a data point, not a beneficiary. |
| Executive                  | Marginal benefit                                                                        | Uses the product for governance narrative. A means to an end.                                                                                                                                                                 |
| Occupational health        | Genuine secondary benefit                                                               | A legitimate professional tool if methodology is sound.                                                                                                                                                                       |
| Privacy-conscious employee | Does not benefit                                                                        | Actively harmed if the tool reduces trust in the employer.                                                                                                                                                                    |
| Union rep                  | Neutral to negative                                                                     | Tasked with protecting members from a tool they did not ask for.                                                                                                                                                              |

### The hardest truth:

The product's stated mission is to help employees. But the person who pays for it is the employer. The employer's primary motivation is almost never "help employees feel better." It is some combination of:

1. **Reduce turnover costs** (the most honest and common reason)
2. **Limit legal and regulatory risk** (growing motivation in jurisdictions with psychosocial safety legislation)
3. **Competitive employer branding** (we care about our people)
4. **Compliance with emerging regulation** (ISO 45003, EU psychosocial directives, SafeWork Australia)

If the product delivers on the employer's actual motivation (lower turnover, reduced risk, better brand) while also genuinely helping employees, it succeeds. If it delivers on the employer's motivation at the expense of employees (surveillance disguised as care), it will face backlash that destroys the brand.

The design challenge is not technical. It is aligning the incentives of the buyer with the needs of the user. Every product decision should be tested against this question: "Does this feature serve the employee's interest, or does it serve the employer's desire to monitor?"

## 5. Adoption Journey — Who Needs to Say "Yes" in What Order

### Enterprise (1000+ employees)

**Phase 1: Internal Champion (Weeks 1-4)**

- HR Director or People Ops lead sees a demo, reads a case study, or attends a conference session
- They bring it to the CHRO informally: "I want to evaluate this"
- CHRO says "look into it" (soft yes, no budget commitment yet)
- Who must say yes: HR Director + CHRO (informal)

**Phase 2: Evaluation (Weeks 4-10)**

- HR team evaluates 2-3 vendors
- IT security does a vendor risk assessment (this is where uncertified products die)
- Legal reviews data handling, privacy, and liability implications
- Works council or union is notified (in unionized or EU environments; this can add 4-8 weeks)
- Who must say yes: HR Director + IT Security + Legal + (Union/Works Council if applicable)

**Phase 3: Budget Approval (Weeks 10-14)**

- CHRO presents business case to CFO or executive team
- ROI model: retention improvement, reduced disability claims, compliance positioning
- CFO pushes on cost. Buyer pushes on risk of inaction.
- Executive team approves budget (or does not)
- Who must say yes: CHRO + CFO + CEO (for budget over threshold)

**Phase 4: Pilot (Weeks 14-22)**

- Deploy to one department or location (often the one with the highest visible turnover)
- Employee communication campaign (this is where privacy positioning makes or breaks adoption)
- Opt-in or soft-launch (opt-out risks immediate backlash; opt-in risks low adoption)
- Who must say yes: Pilot department managers + participating employees

**Phase 5: Full Rollout Decision (Weeks 22-30)**

- Pilot results reviewed: adoption rates, signal quality, action taken, employee feedback
- If adoption is above 40% and no incidents: proceed
- If adoption is below 20% or there was a privacy incident: reassess or cancel
- Who must say yes: CHRO + pilot department feedback + IT (for integration)

**Phase 6: Renewal (Month 12)**

- Did anything change? (retention, absence rates, employee survey scores)
- Is the product being used or ignored?
- Who must say yes: CHRO + whoever controls the budget for year two

**Typical timeline from first touch to full deployment: 6-9 months.**
**Most common failure point: Phase 2 (IT/Legal block) or Phase 4 (employees refuse to engage).**

---

### Mid-Market (100-1000 employees)

**Phase 1: Champion (Weeks 1-2)**

- HR Director or CEO sees the product
- Decision is faster because fewer stakeholders
- Who must say yes: HR Director + CEO/COO

**Phase 2: Evaluation (Weeks 2-4)**

- Lighter security review (may be a checklist, not a full assessment)
- Legal may or may not be involved (depends on the company)
- No union in most cases
- Who must say yes: HR Director + maybe IT + maybe external legal counsel

**Phase 3: Purchase (Weeks 4-6)**

- Shorter budget process. CEO may approve directly.
- Who must say yes: CEO/COO (budget authority)

**Phase 4: Deploy (Weeks 6-10)**

- Often company-wide from the start (no pilot phase)
- Higher risk of rollout problems because there is no learning phase
- Who must say yes: All managers (soft buy-in) + employees (participation)

**Typical timeline: 2-3 months.**
**Most common failure point: Phase 4. No pilot means no opportunity to fix communication problems before they affect the whole company.**

---

### SMB (under 100 employees)

**Phase 1: Decision (Week 1)**

- CEO/COO decides. Often alone.
- May consult one trusted manager or external advisor
- Who must say yes: CEO

**Phase 2: Deploy (Weeks 2-4)**

- Company-wide. Minimal process.
- Often announced at a team meeting or via email
- Who must say yes: CEO. Everyone else is informed, not consulted.

**Typical timeline: 2-4 weeks.**
**Most common failure point: The CEO loses interest after month one and stops looking at the data. The product becomes shelfware.**

---

### The Critical Path

Regardless of company size, the non-negotiable sequence is:

1. **HR Champion** must want it (or it never enters the building)
2. **IT Security** must clear it (or it never gets installed)
3. **Legal** must not block it (or it gets shelved on liability grounds)
4. **Budget holder** must fund it (or it stays a proposal)
5. **Employees** must engage with it (or the data is empty and the value collapses)
6. **Managers** must act on signals (or the tool becomes an expensive thermometer that nobody reads)

The product must be designed to survive each gate. Most wellbeing tools are designed for gate 1 (HR loves the demo) and gate 4 (ROI model for the CFO). The gates that kill deployments are gate 2 (security), gate 3 (legal), and gate 5 (employees). Invest design effort accordingly.
